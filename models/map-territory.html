Map vs. Territory

The way you see a situation is not the situation itself. Your mental picture is always an incomplete version of reality.
Key Insights
1
Every belief and mental model is a simplified version of reality. It's useful until you forget it's a simplification and start treating it as fact.
2
An outdated mental model is often worse than none at all. It produces confident action toward a situation that no longer exists.
3
When something keeps surprising you or not working, the problem is usually your picture of the situation, not the situation itself.
Examples

The Stubborn Investor

Investors often fall in love with their thesis about a company so much that when new information contradicts it, they dismiss the information rather than update the thesis. The thesis is the map. The company's actual performance is the territory. When those two diverge, it's always the map that needs updating.
Origin
The phrase "the map is not the territory" was coined by Alfred Korzybski, a Polish-American scientist and philosopher, in a 1931 paper and later expanded in his 1933 book Science and Sanity. Korzybski developed it as part of his field of general semantics, arguing that human beings too often confuse their abstractions and symbols for the things those abstractions represent. The idea was later adopted widely across philosophy, cognitive science, and systems thinking, and became a foundational concept in NLP and decision-making literature.
