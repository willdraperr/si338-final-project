Circle of Competence

Know where your knowledge actually ends. Operating just past that line is where most bad decisions happen.
Key Insights
1
The danger isn't ignorance, it's false confidence. Most bad decisions happen just past the edge of what you actually understand, not far outside it.
2
Familiarity and competence feel identical from the inside. Something can feel well within your wheelhouse while actually being just outside it.
3
You don't have to avoid unfamiliar territory. You just have to know when you're in it, so you can slow down and ask for help.
Examples

Warren Buffett

Warren Buffett famously avoids investing in technology companies, not because he thinks they're bad businesses, but because he genuinely doesn't feel confident he understands how they'll look in 20 years. While others were piling into tech stocks, he stuck to businesses he could actually evaluate. That restraint is the whole model in action.

The New Manager

Someone who is great at their job gets promoted into managing a team. The skills that made them excellent at the job don't automatically transfer to managing people, but it's easy to assume they do. Recognizing that management is a genuinely different skill set, and that you're now a beginner again, is Circle of Competence applied to your own career.
Origin
The concept was popularized by Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger, who made it central to their investment philosophy at Berkshire Hathaway. Buffett has described it repeatedly in shareholder letters and interviews as one of the most important rules governing their decisions: stay within what you genuinely understand and be ruthlessly honest about where that boundary sits. The underlying idea has older roots in philosophy and epistemology, particularly in Socratic thought, where knowing the limits of one's own knowledge was considered the beginning of wisdom.
