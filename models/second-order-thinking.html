Second-Order Thinking

Before deciding, ask "and then what?" Most problems come from only thinking one step ahead.
Key Insights
1
Most decisions look fine until you ask what happens next. The problems almost always live one or two steps past where people stop thinking.
2
In any situation involving other people, your action changes how they behave. A decision that looks good alone can backfire once others respond to it.
3
The less reversible a decision is, the further ahead you should think. When you can't undo it, the downstream effects actually matter.
Examples

Highway Expansion

A city decides to add more lanes to a congested highway. First-order effect: more road space. Second-order effect: more road space makes driving more attractive, so more people drive, and within a few years the highway is just as congested as before. The solution looked obvious until you asked "and then what?"
Origin
Second-order thinking has roots in systems thinking and cybernetics, fields that emerged in the mid-20th century to study how complex systems behave over time. The economist Frédéric Bastiat touched on the idea in 1850 with his concept of the "seen and the unseen," arguing that good economic reasoning must account for consequences that aren't immediately visible. The term was later popularized in investing and decision-making circles by Howard Marks, whose memos for Oaktree Capital made second-order thinking a cornerstone of sophisticated investment reasoning.
