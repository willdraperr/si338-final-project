Occam's Razor

When you have multiple explanations for something, start with the simplest one that fits the facts.
Key Insights
1
When you have competing explanations and no proof yet, start with the one that requires the fewest assumptions. It's usually right.
2
If an explanation keeps needing new details to hold together, that's a sign the explanation is probably wrong, not that the situation is complicated.
3
In any diagnosis, medical, technical, or personal, rule out the simple explanations first. The boring answer is almost always the correct one.
Examples

Broken Website

Your website traffic suddenly drops. You could theorize that a competitor ran a smear campaign, or that there's a conspiracy among search engines, or that user behavior has fundamentally shifted. Or you could first check whether something broke. Nine times out of ten, something broke. Start there.

The Ignored Email

You send an important email and don't hear back for three days. It's easy to spiral into wondering if you offended them, if they're avoiding you, or if something is wrong with the relationship. Occam's Razor says the simplest explanation is that they're busy or the email got buried. That's almost always what's actually happening.
Origin
The principle is named after William of Ockham, a 14th-century English friar and philosopher. His Latin formulation — roughly translated as "entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity" — appeared throughout his theological and logical writings. He wasn't the first to express the idea (Aristotle and Aquinas touched on similar notions), but his name became attached to it through common usage. The principle became a cornerstone of scientific methodology during the Scientific Revolution and remains a standard tool in philosophy of science today.
