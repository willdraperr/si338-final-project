Feedback Loops

In any system, outputs circle back as inputs. That loop is what makes things grow, stabilize, or spiral out of control.
Key Insights
1
Some loops amplify: the more you have, the more you get. Others stabilize: the further things drift, the harder they get pulled back.
2
Small things feeding back into themselves repeatedly can produce outsized results. This is how good habits compound and how ignored problems become crises.
3
When a fix makes a problem worse, a feedback loop is usually why. The system responded to the solution in a way nobody accounted for.
Examples

Instagram's Growth

The more people use a platform like Instagram, the more content gets created, which makes the platform more valuable, which attracts more users, which encourages more content creation. That's a reinforcing feedback loop and it's exactly how platforms grow explosively in their early stages. The loop feeds itself.

Learning a Skill

When you're learning a new skill and getting regular feedback, small improvements lead to more confidence, which leads to more practice, which leads to more improvement. The loop compounds in your favor. Cut off the feedback, and the loop breaks. This is why tracking progress matters so much when building a habit.
Origin
The formal study of feedback loops emerged from cybernetics, a field founded by mathematician Norbert Wiener in the late 1940s to study self-regulating systems. Wiener's 1948 book Cybernetics established feedback as a foundational concept in understanding how systems maintain stability or spiral out of control. The concept was later expanded through systems thinking by Jay Forrester at MIT and popularized broadly by Peter Senge in his 1990 book The Fifth Discipline, which brought feedback loop thinking into management and organizational theory.
