Survivorship Bias

When you only study what succeeded, you're missing most of the picture. The failures have just as much to teach you.
Key Insights
1
The examples available to learn from are almost never a fair sample. The failures disappeared and left the successes looking like the whole story.
2
Failure tends to be quiet and success tends to be loud. That imbalance means the lessons most available to you are also the most skewed.
3
The fix isn't to ignore success stories. It's to actively look for what happened to everyone who tried the same thing and didn't make it.
Examples
The Famous Dropout
We hear constantly about college dropouts who built billion-dollar companies. What we don't hear about are the thousands of people who dropped out with the same conviction and never caught a break. The dropout success story is visible and memorable. The dropout struggle is quiet and forgotten. If you use the famous stories to conclude that dropping out is a good strategy, you're drawing a lesson from an unrepresentative sample.
Business Advice
Most business advice comes from successful companies explaining what they did. But plenty of failed companies did the exact same things and it didn't work for them. If you only read success stories, you'll start to see patterns that aren't actually predictive, because you never checked whether the same patterns showed up in the failures too.
Origin
The bias was formally identified and named by statistician Abraham Wald during World War II. The military asked Wald to analyze damage patterns on returning aircraft to determine where to add armor. The intuitive answer was to reinforce the most damaged areas, but Wald recognized that the planes being studied were only the ones that made it back. The missing data was the planes that were shot down, and the undamaged areas on returning planes were likely where the fatal hits occurred on the ones that didn't return. His insight became a landmark example of the bias and is still the most cited illustration of it today.
