Inversion

Instead of planning for success, ask what would make this fail and work backward from that.
Key Insights
1
Before committing to a plan, ask what would guarantee it fails. Those answers are cheaper to find on paper than in real life.
2
The more confident you feel, the more important this is. Confidence is exactly when you stop looking for what could go wrong.
3
Feeling stuck on how to improve something? Ask what makes it worse instead. That question is usually easier to answer and just as useful.
Examples
Charlie Munger
Charlie Munger was once asked how to have a good life. His answer was to first make a list of everything that leads to a miserable life, then just avoid doing those things. Obvious in hindsight, but most people never think to flip the question.
Origin
Inversion as a deliberate thinking tool has roots in Stoic philosophy. Marcus Aurelius and Seneca both practiced premeditatio malorum, the deliberate visualization of what could go wrong as a way to prepare and reduce fear. In mathematics, inversion is a formal technique used to solve problems by reversing them. The modern popularization of inversion as a general mental model is largely credited to Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's longtime partner at Berkshire Hathaway, who wrote and spoke extensively about it as one of the core tools in his thinking toolkit.
