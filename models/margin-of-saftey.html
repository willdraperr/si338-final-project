Margin of Safety

Always leave more room for error than you think you need. Things rarely go exactly as planned.
Key Insights
1
Every estimate you make contains error. A margin of safety is just the gap between your projection and the point where being wrong actually hurts you.
2
The bigger the stakes and the less certain your estimates, the larger the buffer should be. Scale the cushion to the cost of being wrong.
3
In competitive situations, cutting the buffer feels like an advantage. But efficiency and resilience trade off, and a system with no slack can't absorb surprises.
Examples

Bridge Engineering

When engineers design a bridge rated for 10,000 pounds, they actually build it to hold 50,000 pounds. Not because they expect that load, but because estimates have error, materials aren't perfect, and unexpected stress happens. The gap between what they planned for and what it can actually handle is the margin of safety. Without it, any surprise becomes a catastrophe.

Project Timelines

If you're planning a project that you estimate will take three weeks, telling your client it'll be done in three weeks leaves zero room for anything to go wrong. Build in a buffer. Not because you're planning to be slow, but because something always comes up, and the buffer is what keeps one unexpected problem from blowing the whole timeline.
Origin
The concept was formalized in investing by Benjamin Graham, widely considered the father of value investing, in his 1949 book The Intelligent Investor. Graham used it to describe the practice of buying assets at a significant discount to their intrinsic value, creating a cushion against errors in valuation. Warren Buffett later called it the three most important words in investing. The principle itself extends well beyond finance into engineering, where it has been standard practice for centuries to design structures and systems to withstand loads significantly greater than the maximum expected demand.
